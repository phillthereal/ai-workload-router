Assumptions & caveats

How this model works

  • Formula: net savings ≈ (easy_fraction × cost_share_factor × (1 − 1/price_range)) − overhead. cost_share_factor (fixed at 0.90 for the classifier, 1.30 for the cascade — the cascade tries the cheap model on everything and only escalates failures, so it captures savings on borderline tasks a predict-then-route classifier would conservatively send straight to the frontier) is not a slider; it's baked in from the live benchmark's task-mix.
  • Overhead is a flat cost, not a cut of savings. The classifier/verifier call still runs — and still costs money — even when there's no easy work to find. That's the entire reason the negative-savings case exists: on an all-hard workload, savings collapse toward zero but the overhead doesn't, so the net goes negative. Overhead is interpolated between the single-vendor and cross-vendor measured values by your price-range input.
  • Cascade + cross-vendor is extrapolated, not measured. Every live cascade number in the source benchmark is single-vendor. Its cross-vendor overhead is estimated from the classifier's cross-vs-single overhead ratio — treat cascade results at high price ranges as more speculative than everything else on this page.
  • Verdict thresholds are a judgment call: ≥20% net → "pays for itself," 0–20% → "marginal," below 0% → negative. Real anchors cluster at 40–65% for genuinely easy-skewed workloads, so 20% is a deliberately conservative bar, not the midpoint of observed results.
  • One decimal, always. Nothing here is precise to the second decimal — the underlying benchmark is 25 tasks. Treat every number on this page as directional, calibrated on a 25-task live benchmark; n is small.
  • The cascade's overhead can be cut further with a cheap (budget-tier) verifier instead of an independent one — down to ≈6% of savings — but that configuration is quality-unsafe on hard traffic (it can't reliably catch its own model's failures) and isn't modeled here as a separate mode.
