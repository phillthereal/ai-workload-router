The problem

Everyone routes to the premium model "to be safe."

Teams shipping features on large language models tend to send every request to one top-tier model, because comparing models task-by-task is tedious and there's no cheap way to know which jobs a smaller model can handle. The bill scales linearly with usage, and there's no visibility into the price-for-quality tradeoff being made on every single call.

The person who feels this is the engineer or founder who owns that bill — with only one blunt lever available: "switch everything to a cheaper model and hope quality holds."

The bet worth testing

One hypothesis, everything else deferred.

The original concept was expansive — a multi-agent orchestrator splitting tasks across four models, adaptive routing, a full web app, three build phases. Most of that is machinery you'd build before knowing whether the core idea even pays off. I cut it to a single testable claim:

For a realistic task mix, routing to cheaper models where they suffice cuts cost meaningfully while holding quality within an acceptable band of the frontier-only baseline.

If that's false, no orchestration UI matters. So v1 proves it on a benchmark first. Task decomposition, the multi-agent layer, the frontend, adaptive routing — all explicitly deferred, not deleted.

Defining "done" before building

A named baseline and a quality floor.

A baseline, because "reduce cost" is meaningless without one. The reference is every task sent to the frontier model (Claude Opus 4.8); all savings are measured against it. A quality floor, because cheap answers that are wrong are worthless. Success required holding at least 95% of baseline quality — measured by an LLM judge, not assumed.

The insight that matters

The savings ceiling is set by your workload — and your vendor range.

The hypothesis set a 40% target. The first, all-Anthropic run landed at 32.5%; this cross-vendor run landed at 53.6% — and why the ceiling moved is the real product lesson. Look at the reasoning row above: zero savings, in both runs. Those tasks correctly route to the frontier model under both strategies, because a cheaper model measurably underperforms on them.

Reasoning tasks are 16% of the tasks but ~33% of the baseline cost — and that third of the bill is locked. No routing can reduce it without losing quality. What moved is everything else: swapping the budget and mid tiers onto OpenAI and DeepSeek widened the price range the router works with, and the savings on the unlocked two-thirds grew from 32.5% to 53.6%.

That arc — a roadmap line ("cross-provider routing widens the ceiling") executed and confirmed — is the proof. So the levers for anyone adopting this are traffic composition (fixed, sets what's locked) and vendor price range (a choice you control). A product that's 80% classification will save far more than one that's half hard reasoning; widening the vendor range you route across raises the ceiling further, exactly as this run shows. The performance log makes the composition side visible, which is the entire point. That's a more honest and more useful finding than a suspiciously round 40% would have been.

V2 — the roadmap, executed

Three strategies beat the target. A harder workload reset it.

The "what I'd build next" list below isn't hypothetical anymore. I replaced the hand-labeled task difficulty with a real prompt classifier, built the confidence-based escalation cascade, and ran a second benchmark built specifically to find where routing stops paying. All three new strategies cleared the original 40% target.

Net savings by strategy — vs. frontier-only baseline, cross-vendor unless noted

Prompt classifier cross-vendor roster · net of classification cost 65.6%
Cascade escalate-on-failure · cheap model first, Opus only if it fails a quality gate 53.1%
Within-vendor tiering Claude-only ladder · Haiku → Sonnet → Opus 42.5%

The classifier beat the original hand-labeled v1 run (53.6%) — it found seven cheaper routes the conservative hand labels missed, all judged 0.85–1.00, for a routing cost of about 1% of the savings. The cascade beat the predict-ahead classifier within a single vendor (53.1% vs. 42.5%) by trying the cheap model on everything and reacting to what fails, instead of pre-routing hard work to the frontier.

On a harder task set that actually separates model quality tiers, routing goes cost-negative — but it never sacrifices output quality.

The published 25-task benchmark saturates quality near 0.99 on every tier, so it can't test whether routing holds up when tasks are genuinely hard. A new 10-task hard set — mostly objective, exact-match, no judge leniency — separates the tiers cleanly:

Model tierHard-set quality
Haiku · budget0.70
Sonnet · mid0.90
Opus · frontier1.00

On that hard set, every strategy still held 100% of baseline quality — but cost more than sending everything straight to Opus, because every task genuinely needed the frontier model and routing only added overhead. That's the sharper form of the v1 composition insight: the router's savings are a direct function of how much of the workload is actually easy. On an all-hard workload there's no free lunch — but the quality guarantee holds either way, which is the more important half of that sentence. The buyer's decision rule falls out of it: measure your easy-task fraction before adopting a router.

What I'd build next

From proof to product.

  1. 1Adaptive routing. Feed the performance log back into the router so model choice is learned per task type, not hand-configured. The log was designed for this from day one.
  2. 2Confidence-based escalation. Try the cheap model first; auto-retry on a stronger one only when the judge score is low — more savings without breaching the quality floor.
  3. 3More vendors. Cross-provider routing is now proven across three (Anthropic, OpenAI, DeepSeek); a fourth — e.g. a Gemini adapter — is the next test of whether the ceiling keeps climbing or starts to plateau.
  4. 4Judge-vs-human validation. The cross-judge check passed (96.7% agreement); next is scoring the exported human label sheet against both judges, and growing past 25 tasks, before quoting absolute quality numbers as precise.

V3 — the router learns from its own log

The router now learns from its own history. On repeat task shapes it beats the static router by 22.7% at identical quality — and costs nothing to consult.

Every run so far — task type, model, cost, quality — sits in data/runs.db, previously unused for routing decisions. v3 is built and evaluated: consult that history before routing, and only downgrade a task when the evidence clears a quality bar — the same never-trade-quality guarantee as v1/v2. Held out on 14 tasks the log had never seen (chronological split, no leakage from a task's own batch or the future):

Held-out cost, 14 tasks — static classifier vs. learned router, identical 1.000 quality both arms

Static router cold classifier, no history · 4.4% net saving vs. frontier $0.018516
Learned router consults data/runs.db first · 26.1% net saving vs. frontier $0.014319

↓ 22.7% cheaper than the static router at identical quality (1.000 vs. 1.000) — and the lookup is SQL over the run log, not an LLM call, so routing overhead is $0. 9 of 14 tasks downgraded on strong evidence (all scored 1.00); 3 downgrades were refused where history showed the cheap tier failing that exact shape (stayed at the frontier, scored 1.00); 2 fell back to the static classifier on thin history (cold start).

The catch, and it has to travel with the number: this saving is conditional on the workload repeating task shapes it's already logged. The held-out set was written to repeat shapes seen ≥5 times before — the only regime a learned router can act in. A workload with no repeated shapes gets cold start everywhere: exactly the static router's numbers, at the same $0 overhead. Full write-up: docs/V3_FINDINGS.md (design: docs/V3_DESIGN.md).

Honest limitations

What this does and doesn't prove.

  • The judge is validated, not proven. LLM-as-judge scoring is now cross-checked by a second, independent judge from a different vendor (96.7% agreement, 0.010 mean difference) — but both are still LLMs and could share blind spots a human grader wouldn't. A human label sheet is exported; scoring it is the remaining step.
  • 25 tasks is a starting eval, enough to demonstrate the method and the composition insight — not to certify a production savings figure.
  • Single-turn tasks only. Real workloads include multi-turn and tool-use flows this benchmark doesn't cover.
  • The at-scale projection is directional. It's a linear extrapolation from this run's per-task cost and assumes production traffic resembles this benchmark's mix — real traffic will differ.
